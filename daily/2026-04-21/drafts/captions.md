## LinkedIn (target 200–300 words · analytical register · "why this matters" framing)

The United States committed more than twenty-three times as much private capital to AI as China did in 2025. American firms produced most of the notable frontier models. On every supply-side measure in Stanford's 2026 AI Index, the US leads by a wide margin.

On adoption, it ranks 24th.

The Microsoft AI Economy Institute surveyed 30 economies in the second half of 2025. The United Arab Emirates came in first at 64.0%. Singapore followed at 60.9%. Norway, Ireland, France, Spain, and New Zealand all cleared 40%. The UK, Netherlands, Qatar, Australia, Israel, Belgium, Canada, Switzerland, Sweden, Austria, and South Korea all cleared 30%.

The United States sat at 28.3% — middle of the pack, trailing most other high-income economies on the list. Behind Taiwan. Behind Poland. Behind Germany.

South Korea had the fastest change of any country over the year: 25.9% in the first half of 2025, 30.7% in the second, climbing seven ranks in six months. The United States gained 2.0 percentage points over the same window; its rank did not move.

Why this matters for product teams and policy desks: the standard "AI race" narrative weighs invention, investment, and compute. Those are not the same as use. A technology that diffuses through smartphone and browser will land where people reach for it. The leaderboard of who builds AI and the leaderboard of who uses AI are not the same shape.

*Ollie's verdict: the country that wrote the menu is eating somewhere else.*

*— Ollie*
