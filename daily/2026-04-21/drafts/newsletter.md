# The country that wrote the menu is eating somewhere else

*Issue 03 · Data · 21 April 2026*

The United States ranks 24th at using generative AI.

Microsoft's AI Economy Institute surveyed 30 high- and upper-middle-income economies in the second half of 2025. The United Arab Emirates came in first, at 64% of the adult population using generative AI tools. Singapore followed at 60.9%. Then Norway, Ireland, France, Spain, and New Zealand — all above 40%. The UK, Netherlands, Qatar, Australia, Israel, Belgium, Canada, Switzerland, Sweden, Austria, and South Korea all cleared 30%.

The United States sat at 28.3%. Behind Taiwan. Behind Poland. Behind Germany.

## Supply and demand have different leaderboards

On every supply-side metric Stanford's 2026 AI Index tracks, the United States dominates. American firms committed $285.9 billion of private AI capital in 2025 — 23 times China's figure. American companies produced most of the notable frontier models. The country hosts 5,427 data centres, more than ten times any other nation. The compute, the capital, the chip supply chain (via TSMC), the talent concentration — all cluster on US soil.

That's the leaderboard for invention. The leaderboard for use is different.

The Microsoft data, which Stanford reproduces on page 201 of the report, shows adoption correlating strongly with GDP per capita — but not cleanly. The UAE and Singapore outperform what their income would predict. Denmark and the United States underperform. Something in the American daily routine hasn't reached for the software the country exported.

## South Korea moved

South Korea had the fastest adoption shift of any country over the year: 25.9% in the first half of 2025, 30.7% in the second. That is seven ranks climbed in six months, from 25th to 18th. Still ahead of the United States.

The US gained 2.0 percentage points over the same window. Its rank didn't move.

## Why the shape matters

The standard "AI race" framing weighs invention, investment, and compute. Those are not the same as use. Generative AI diffuses through the phone in your pocket and the browser in your tab — the same surfaces everyone already has. Gaps in adoption aren't gaps in access. They are gaps in reach.

The shape of the leaderboard — Gulf monarchies and Paris above San Francisco — is the opposite of what a supply-side read would predict. A race you invent and don't run is not a race you win.
