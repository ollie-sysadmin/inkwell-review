## Angle 1: The world's major river deltas are sinking faster than the seas are rising

- **News peg:** Nature-published global study, released 20 April 2026, covering every large delta on Earth — local subsidence is dwarfing the climate sea-level-rise signal in most of the world's delta megacities.
- **Format:** 🗺️ Cartography
- **Data availability:** Strong — the study itself publishes the per-delta subsidence rates (mm/yr) alongside IPCC sea-level-rise projections. Mekong, Ganges-Brahmaputra, Nile, Pearl River, Mississippi, Niger, Chao Phraya all covered.
- **Freshness window:** 1 week, but the story has long-tail SEO value.
- **Sharpness score:** 5 — the counterintuitive finding is that for most megadelta cities (Jakarta, Dhaka, Alexandria, Ho Chi Minh City, New Orleans), ground subsidence is 2–10× the sea-level signal. Climate coverage almost never shows this split.
- **Why it works:** Inkwell's job is exactly this kind of "the chart reveals something the dominant narrative obscures." Map the two rates side by side on one choropleth and the story is told instantly.
- **Risk:** climate stories are oversaturated in general, but *subsidence-vs-sea-level* framing is not. Has to be written with care not to read as climate-denialist (the data is: both matter, local subsidence dominates most places).
- **Evergreen queue match:** none exact. Candidate to add to the queue regardless.

## Angle 2: States that legalised sports betting, and what happened to their credit scores

- **News peg:** study cited this week showing that state-level legalisation of online sports betting is followed by measurable drops in credit scores, rises in bad debt, and rises in bankruptcy filings.
- **Format:** ▦ Grid (state-by-state) or 🔍 Specimen (one state's before-and-after dissected). Could also work as 📈 Life tracking years-since-legalisation.
- **Data availability:** Strong — Fed consumer credit data by state, bankruptcy filings by state (public), legalisation dates public. The underlying study is the source line; we chart the mechanism.
- **Freshness window:** evergreen with a topical hook. The story doesn't go stale if we ship mid-week.
- **Sharpness score:** 5 — legalisation was sold as economic upside. The data says otherwise for household finances. That's the definition of "worth repeating at dinner."
- **Why it works:** most-shareable ratio is very high. Plays directly to the journalist flywheel (bookmarkable by personal-finance writers). SEO-rich (lots of high-intent queries around sports-betting regulation).
- **Risk:** must be framed as observation, not advocacy. Avoid the moralising tone the voice bible explicitly forbids.
- **Evergreen queue match:** none. Strong candidate to add.

## Angle 3: The Strait of Hormuz, and every other chokepoint one crisis could close

- **News peg:** Iran's closure of the Strait of Hormuz this week after US Navy seized an Iranian cargo ship; Iran-US ceasefire expiring 22 April.
- **Format:** 🗺️ Cartography
- **Data availability:** Strong — EIA's World Oil Transit Chokepoints report is the canonical source (Hormuz, Bab el-Mandeb, Suez, Panama, Malacca, Turkish Straits, Cape of Good Hope). Volume + % of global crude + LNG through each.
- **Freshness window:** 1 week max.
- **Sharpness score:** 3 — the numbers are arresting (Hormuz ~20% of global oil) but this map has been produced many times. Inkwell can do it better (photocopy grammar, per-chokepoint stamp), but it isn't a novel angle.
- **Why it works:** timely, visually arresting, the data carries itself. Fast to produce if we pick this.
- **Risk:** oversaturated. Every major outlet will publish a version of this chart.
- **Evergreen queue match:** none. Could add as a Priority B.

## Angle 4: How much of America is now owned by Wall Street

- **News peg:** Senate housing bill this week restricting large institutional investors from owning single-family homes.
- **Format:** 🗺️ Cartography (state- or metro-level) or 📉 Shrinkage (share of first-time-buyer transactions since 2010).
- **Data availability:** Medium — NAR, CoreLogic, and Invitation Homes disclosures give a national picture; zip-code concentration in Atlanta, Charlotte, Phoenix is well-documented.
- **Freshness window:** 1–2 weeks. Housing policy moves slowly; the peg holds.
- **Sharpness score:** 4 — the national share is smallish (~2–3%), but concentration in specific metros (some zip codes >25%) is genuinely non-obvious.
- **Why it works:** speaks directly to a lived behavioural reality — why first-time buyers are losing bids. Strong "show the hot spots" cartography angle.
- **Risk:** definitions matter (what counts as "institutional"? 10+ homes? 100+? 1000+?). Source and footnote with care or the chart is contested.
- **Evergreen queue match:** closest is #6 Billionaire Residences, but distinct enough to warrant its own entry.

- **Format:** 🗺️ Cartography
- **Data availability:** Medium — Japan's Headquarters for Earthquake Research Promotion publishes 30-year probabilities for every major subduction segment; USGS has global seismic-gap data. Enough to chart.
- **Freshness window:** 1 week.
- **Sharpness score:** 3 — the 30-year Nankai probability (70–80%) is striking, but "where earthquakes might hit" is a well-known map shape. The novel angle is the probability layer, not the geography.

- **Risk:** probabilistic-seismology territory — must not frame as "prediction." Chart shows historical recurrence intervals + current probability estimates, full stop.

The finding — that subsidence dwarfs sea-level rise in the places that matter — is exactly the "worth repeating at dinner" test. Cartography template is battle-tested, so production risk is low. The Nature study landed yesterday, giving us a 3–5 day sweet spot before every climate publication catches up.

**Alternate if time is tight: Angle 2 (sports betting).** Equally sharp, but Grid format needs a template build — roughly doubles the work. Hold this one for next session unless Dene wants to start on Template #2 now.

**Skip Angle 3 (Hormuz).** Oversaturated — every outlet will publish a version. Inkwell's edge is showing something others aren't.
