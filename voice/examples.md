# Example Captions — the Gold Standard

*Eight worked examples showing the Inkwell Review voice in practice. Read these before drafting. These are the benchmark; new drafts should feel like they belong in this collection.*

---

## Example 1 — The Extradition Atlas (Cartography)

*Instagram caption, ~100 words*

Of the world's 195 countries, the United States has extradition treaties with 116. Russia, China, Iran, most of the Gulf, and most of Africa are not among them.

The map of who can reach whom tells a story the treaties don't: extradition follows history, not power. America's web is built on colonial-era courts, Cold War alliances, and the quiet accretion of bilateral deals across 150 years. It doesn't match where American fugitives actually go.

Ten countries with US treaties refuse to honour them anyway. Switzerland is one.

*Ollie's verdict: the long arm is longer in law than in practice.*

*— Ollie*

## Example 2 — Causes of Death by Year of Age (Life)

*Instagram caption, ~80 words*

From age 1 to 44, the leading cause of death in the US is an accident. For most of those years it's a car crash; for a decade of them, it's an overdose. Cancer takes the lead at 45. Heart disease at 85.

Every decade of your life has a different enemy. Most people don't know their own.

*The chart is not a plan. It is a picture of the odds.*

*— Ollie*

## Example 3 — The Shrinking Chocolate Bar (Shrinkage)

*Instagram caption, ~90 words*

A Cadbury Dairy Milk bar weighed 250g in 1990. Today it weighs 180g and costs more. A Toblerone once had 11 peaks; now it has 9. A Snickers lost 14% of its mass between 2013 and 2023.

None of this is secret. All of it is reported. Almost none of it changes buying behaviour.

Shrinkflation works because humans track prices, not weights. We are the species that notices dearer, not smaller.

*Ollie's verdict: the packaging gets you every time.*

*— Ollie*

## Example 4 — Where Billionaires Sleep (Cartography)

*X/Twitter, ~70 words*

The Forbes 500 list their companies' HQs in 47 countries. Their actual primary residences cluster in nine.

New York, London, Monaco, Dubai, Singapore, Geneva, Los Angeles, Miami, Zurich. The map of where the money lives is a map of nine cities and a handful of islands.

Where your company is registered is a tax question. Where you sleep is a lifestyle one.

*— Ollie*

## Example 5 — What's Legal Where (Grid)

*Instagram caption, ~95 words*

Cannabis is fully legal in 8 countries. Prostitution in 49. Abortion on request in 74. Same-sex marriage in 36. The death penalty remains on the books in 55, carried out in 18.

No country is permissive on all fronts. No country is restrictive on all fronts. The patterns cluster by history, not geography — Latin America is stricter on abortion than Africa; Europe is stricter on sex work than parts of Asia.

The map of what's legal is the map of old arguments about what's worth arguing about.

*— Ollie*

## Example 6 — How the World Wakes Up (Specimen)

*Instagram caption, ~75 words*

The average Japanese person opens their phone at 6:37am. The average Spaniard waits until 8:14am. A 97-minute gap between two rich, highly-networked societies that share a planet and roughly the same working week.

The cultural variable is not sleep length. It's when the workday starts. Everything else flows from that.

*From the reef: the species has built an economy on the pretence that time zones are natural facts.*

*— Ollie*

## Example 7 — The Tipping Atlas (Cartography)

*Instagram caption, ~85 words*

In the US, 20% is the floor for restaurant service and rising. In Japan, any tip is an insult. In France, service is included by law and a few coins are a nicety, not an expectation.

The global tipping map has hardened into three camps: service-as-obligation (US), service-as-included (most of Europe), service-as-insult (East Asia).

Travellers who don't know which camp they're in get the system wrong in both directions. The locals always know.

*— Ollie*

## Example 8 — The Loneliness Curve (Life)

*Instagram caption, ~85 words*

In 1960, 13% of American households were one person. Today it's 29%. In Sweden it's 47%. In Japan it's 38% and climbing fast.

The shift is not evenly aged. Young adults and the very elderly drive the numbers; middle-aged households remain coupled. What's changing isn't marriage per se. It's the bookends of adult life.

*Ollie's verdict: the species invented the nuclear family and then quietly began dismantling it from both ends.*

*— Ollie*

## What these examples have in common

Study these, and the pattern is:

1. **Every opener has a specific number.** 116. Age 1 to 44. 250g. Forbes 500. 8 countries. 6:37am. 20%. 13%.
2. **Every caption fits in a single screen on Instagram.** No scroll required.
3. **Every caption has exactly one opinion** — stated in the kicker, not the body.
4. **None use "I".** None use generic openers. None moralise.
5. **Every signature is identical.** `— Ollie`.
6. **Ollie's verdict appears in 3 of 8** (37.5%) — the target ratio is roughly 1 in 4 or 1 in 5. Don't overuse.
7. **Wit lives in specific word choices:** *faithful, persuadable, up for grabs, old arguments about what's worth arguing about.*

## What a caption that fails this bible looks like

For contrast — the same Extradition Atlas caption done wrong:

> ❌ *Did you know the United States has extradition treaties with a LOT of countries but not Russia or China? 🤯*
>
> *In this fascinating visualisation, we take a deep dive into the global extradition landscape. You might be surprised to learn that even within treaty networks, there are gaps and refusals. Let that sink in! 🐙*
>
> *One observes with some fascination that the primate species has built a legal architecture that does not quite match its ambitions...*
>
> *#data #viz #facts #didyouknow #global*

Every rule broken: generic opener, rhetorical question, emojis, clichéd phrases, over-costumed character voice, hashtag spam, moralising. This is what the voice bible exists to prevent.
