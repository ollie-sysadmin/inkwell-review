# The Inkwell Review Voice Bible

*Read this every time before drafting a caption. The single most important file in the project.*

---

## The one-sentence summary

Inkwell Review reads like a smart, funny, culturally-literate friend who works with data — with Ollie the octopus as a signature flourish, not a costume.

## The seasoning principle

90% of every caption is plain sharp English. 10% is Ollie.

Too much Ollie = costume party. Reader tax too high; audience ceiling too low.
Too little Ollie = generic data viz account #847. No differentiation.
Correct amount = food that tastes like something.

## Where Ollie appears (seasoning locations)

- The signature line at the end: `— Ollie`
- The logo and masthead (visual only, not verbal)
- An occasional kicker: `Ollie's verdict: …` or `from the reef: …`
- The about page (here he gets the full character treatment)
- Stickers, merch, year-in-review, annual reader letters
- Replies to comments (chance to be in-character with minimal effort)

## Where Ollie does NOT appear

- The body of captions (this is the rule most people get wrong)
- The headline on the chart itself (keep clean and declarative)
- The newsletter body text (keep editorial)
- Pitches to journalists (your own name and voice)
- Sponsor communications (professional register)

## The caption formula

A standard Inkwell Review post caption has this shape:

```
[HOOK] — sharpest number, opening beat.
[CONTEXT] — 1–2 sentences expanding what the data shows.
[KICKER] — one-line closer with a view.
[SIGNATURE] — — Ollie
```

Four short sections. Usually 60–150 words total. Never longer than four paragraphs. If it needs to be longer, it belongs in the newsletter.

## The chart headline formula

Headlines on the charts themselves (not captions) follow a different rule:

- Specific, not clever
- Descriptive of what the chart shows
- No puns, no octopus, no Ollie
- Fraunces display type, sentence case, short

Good: *"Where the Long Arm Reaches"* (for the Extradition Atlas — memorable and descriptive)
Also good: *"The Fugitive Map"* or *"Who Flees Where"*
Bad: *"Ollie Investigates the Extradition Web"* (too character-forward; fights the chart)

## Opening lines — the highest-leverage sentence in every post

The first line carries the whole post. Rules:

- **Lead with the sharpest number or fact.** Numbers are the hook.
- **Be specific.** *"47 trips in 11 years"* beats *"the Pope travels a lot."*
- **No generic openers.** Forbidden openers include:
  - "Did you know…"
  - "It's fascinating that…"
  - "In today's world…"
  - "You might be surprised to learn…"
  - "Let's talk about…"
  - "A thread on…"
- **Start mid-thought sometimes.** A sentence that begins *"Of the 192 countries…"* or *"Only three nations…"* or *"Every year since 1987…"* — all stronger than ceremonial introductions.

## Sentence rhythm

- Short sentences. Plain words.
- One idea per sentence.
- Vary length across a caption: short-short-long-short works. All-short reads staccato; all-long reads tired.
- Read every draft aloud. If you stumble, the reader will too.

## Wit — where it lives and where it doesn't

Wit lives in **word choice**, not sentence structure. A single unexpected word earns more than ten clever clauses.

Examples:
- "The faithful were never the persuadable ones" — *faithful* and *persuadable* are working hard in one tight sentence.
- "Picking fights with the man in white rarely pays" — *the man in white* does the heavy lifting.
- "A fight for an audience that wasn't up for grabs" — straight declarative, but *up for grabs* snaps.

Resist: elaborate metaphors, extended analogies, puns in the body text (save puns for the kicker or the chart title).

---

## Opinions — earned, not projected

Every post should have a point of view. Neutral chart descriptions perform poorly and differentiate nothing.

But opinions must be **earned from the data**. If the chart shows X, you can say X. You cannot say Y because you wish the chart showed it.

A good opinion reads like a conclusion. A bad opinion reads like an argument.

- Earned: *"The faithful were never the persuadable ones"* (the chart shows approval correlates with religiosity, not recency of papal travel — so this is a defensible reading)
- Projected: *"Trump is going to lose evangelical support"* (the chart doesn't show this; it's a prediction dressed up as observation)

## Moralising — forbidden

State the finding. Let readers draw the lesson themselves. Moralising in captions is the fastest way to make a smart post feel cheap.

- Bad: *"This shows how twisted our priorities have become."*
- Good: *"The US spends 16% more on prisons than on primary education."*

The second sentence is more damning than the first *because* it trusts the reader. The reader does the moral work. That's what makes them share it.

## The kicker — one line of Ollie

Every post ends with a one-line closer that serves as the character's moment. Two formats:

**Format A — the inline kicker.** A final declarative sentence that's slightly sharper than the body.

> *The polling has been saying this for forty years.*
>
> *— Ollie*

**Format B — the branded aside.** Explicitly labelled as Ollie speaking.

> *Ollie's verdict: picking fights with the man in white rarely pays.*
>
> *— Ollie*

Use Format B sparingly — roughly one in five posts. Overused it becomes a tic. Format A is the workhorse.

## The signature

Always `— Ollie` (em dash, single word, line of its own). Never:

- ~~— Prof. Inkwell~~
- ~~— Ollie 🐙~~
- ~~— The Editor~~

Consistency is the point. One signature, every time.

## Forbidden phrases & patterns

Things that will make a caption feel like every other data viz account:

- "Let that sink in."
- "The numbers don't lie."
- "Buckle up."
- "Spoiler alert."
- "Plot twist."
- Rhetorical questions as openers: *"Ever wondered why…?"*
- Starting with a rhetorical list: *"Taxes. Death. And…"*
- Em dashes used as interruptions in casual captions (em dashes are fine, but two per caption maximum)
- Emoji anywhere in caption body. Exception: a single emoji in the bio is fine; emojis in posts are not.
- Hashtag spam. Maximum 5 hashtags on Instagram, and each must be specific (#extradition is fine; #data #viz #facts is not).

## What Ollie sounds like when he does show up

On the about page, in merch copy, in year-in-review posts, Ollie can talk. Here's what he sounds like:

> *Prof. Ollie Inkwell is a common octopus (Octopus vulgaris) based in an undisclosed reef. He has been observing the primate species Homo sapiens since 2014. He edits Inkwell Review, published Tuesdays and Fridays.*
>
> *He holds three hearts and no spouse. He finds human behaviour endlessly curious, occasionally alarming, and very rarely boring.*

Restrained. Dry. Specific. Never cartoonish. He is a real character who happens to be an octopus, not an octopus mascot. Subtle but crucial difference.

## How to use this bible

- **Before drafting:** read this file plus `voice/examples.md`. Five minutes, always worth it.
- **After drafting:** run `/voice-check`. Flags drifts back to the rules.
- **Quarterly:** review and update. Add new examples from pieces that landed. Remove rules that turn out not to matter. The voice should evolve slowly; don't freeze it but don't churn it.
